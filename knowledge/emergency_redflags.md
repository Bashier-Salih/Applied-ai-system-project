# Emergency Red Flags

## Symptoms that warrant an immediate vet or emergency clinic visit
Signs such as active bleeding that won't stop, repeated vomiting or vomiting blood, difficulty breathing, collapse, suspected poisoning, a bloated or distended abdomen, seizures, or being unable to urinate should be treated as emergencies. These are not scheduling questions and should never wait for a "next available slot."

## Scope of this assistant
This assistant is a scheduling and general-care helper, not a veterinary diagnostic tool. It cannot examine a pet, run tests, or make a medical diagnosis, and it should never attempt to tell an owner whether a specific symptom is serious. Any question describing an active symptom, injury, or "is this normal/an emergency" concern should be redirected to a licensed veterinarian or emergency animal hospital rather than answered directly.

## Non-emergency but vet-worthy patterns
Gradual changes like decreased appetite over several days, new lethargy, limping that persists more than a day, or a recurring task being skipped repeatedly due to the pet resisting it, are worth mentioning to a vet at a routine visit even though they are not immediate emergencies.

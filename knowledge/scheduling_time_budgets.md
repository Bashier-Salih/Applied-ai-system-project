# Scheduling and Time Budgets

## Prioritizing under a tight budget
When an owner's available_hours is small relative to total task duration, it's generally better to protect high-priority, biologically necessary tasks (feeding, medication, minimum exercise) and let lower-priority enrichment or optional grooming tasks be the ones that get skipped, rather than the reverse.

## Multi-pet households
In households with more than one pet, splitting a fixed daily time budget evenly across pets can under-serve a pet with genuinely higher needs (e.g. a high-energy puppy sharing a budget with a low-activity senior cat). It's worth surfacing when one pet's plan is consistently thin compared to what its life stage or breed would suggest.

## Buffer time
Leaving small buffers (5-10 minutes) between back-to-back tasks, rather than scheduling them edge-to-edge, reduces the chance that a task running slightly long causes a cascade of lateness through the rest of the day.
